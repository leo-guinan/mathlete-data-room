<section class="section section--body section--first section--last" name="1400"><div class="section-divider"><hr class="section-divider"/></div><div class="section-content"><div class="section-inner sectionLayout--insetColumn"><h3 class="graf graf--h3 graf--leading graf--title" id="0e71" name="0e71">Diversity is the Key to Innovation</h3><p class="graf graf--p graf-after--h3" id="b00e" name="b00e">Thinking with Legos</p><figure class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p" id="ff19" name="ff19"><img alt="Lego minifigures emerging from computer keyboard" class="graf-image" data-height="2643" data-image-id="0*pV57Iuw-UcX_bF13" data-is-featured="true" data-unsplash-photo-id="26vBUtlufFo" data-width="3964" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/0*pV57Iuw-UcX_bF13"/><figcaption class="imageCaption">Photo by <a class="markup--anchor markup--figure-anchor" data-href="https://unsplash.com/@jamesponddotco?utm_source=medium&amp;utm_medium=referral" href="https://unsplash.com/@jamesponddotco?utm_source=medium&amp;utm_medium=referral" rel="photo-creator noopener" target="_blank">James Pond</a> on <a class="markup--anchor markup--figure-anchor" data-href="https://unsplash.com?utm_source=medium&amp;utm_medium=referral" href="https://unsplash.com?utm_source=medium&amp;utm_medium=referral" rel="photo-source noopener" target="_blank">Unsplash</a></figcaption></figure><p class="graf graf--p graf-after--figure" id="6381" name="6381">Diversity is seemingly becoming a very charged word lately. It has a lot of emotions associated with it, and rightly so. I understand the emotions around it, but I do want to acknowledge them, and set them aside for this discussion. So I want to differentiate between diversity (the understandings and emotions associated with everything surrounding that word) and Diversity (the specific concept I will define in this post).</p><figure class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p" id="059c" name="059c"><img alt="Sign declaring that all are welcome, regardless of anything" class="graf-image" data-height="5013" data-image-id="0*5kpmLAQdS65Rw0ir" data-unsplash-photo-id="98uYQ-KupiE" data-width="3342" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/0*5kpmLAQdS65Rw0ir"/><figcaption class="imageCaption">Photo by <a class="markup--anchor markup--figure-anchor" data-href="https://unsplash.com/@brittaniburns?utm_source=medium&amp;utm_medium=referral" href="https://unsplash.com/@brittaniburns?utm_source=medium&amp;utm_medium=referral" rel="photo-creator noopener" target="_blank">Brittani Burns</a> on <a class="markup--anchor markup--figure-anchor" data-href="https://unsplash.com?utm_source=medium&amp;utm_medium=referral" href="https://unsplash.com?utm_source=medium&amp;utm_medium=referral" rel="photo-source noopener" target="_blank">Unsplash</a></figcaption></figure><p class="graf graf--p graf-after--figure" id="e7c4" name="e7c4">Innovation is limited by the Diversity of the team working on a given problem. What do I mean by that? The Diversity of a team is the set of all ideas and experiences contained within a team. When multiple team members overlap in shared experiences and ideas, these are technically duplicated. This isn’t always a bad thing, but it can be limiting.</p><p class="graf graf--p graf-after--p" id="8199" name="8199">So that all sounds interesting, but I did promise Legos. So here they are. Imagine a person as a Lego set. They are the sum of all of their ideas and experiences, or said differently, they are made of of all of their little pieces.</p><figure class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p" id="0deb" name="0deb"><img alt="Hands holding a wide array of Lego minifigures" class="graf-image" data-height="4000" data-image-id="0*FASEkFOiIO9AVfMa" data-unsplash-photo-id="1FI2QAYPa-Y" data-width="6000" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/0*FASEkFOiIO9AVfMa"/><figcaption class="imageCaption">Photo by <a class="markup--anchor markup--figure-anchor" data-href="https://unsplash.com/@vladhilitanu?utm_source=medium&amp;utm_medium=referral" href="https://unsplash.com/@vladhilitanu?utm_source=medium&amp;utm_medium=referral" rel="photo-creator noopener" target="_blank">Vlad Hilitanu</a> on <a class="markup--anchor markup--figure-anchor" data-href="https://unsplash.com?utm_source=medium&amp;utm_medium=referral" href="https://unsplash.com?utm_source=medium&amp;utm_medium=referral" rel="photo-source noopener" target="_blank">Unsplash</a></figcaption></figure><p class="graf graf--p graf-after--figure" id="1e5f" name="1e5f">People with a large number of shared ideas and experience are like Legos within a given subset. For example, Star Wars Legos or Lord of the Rings Legos. Lego sets in these groups have a lot of overlapping pieces. So when trying to innovate, you want to see what the possibilities are. When pieces tend to be duplicates, by adding pieces you do increase the number of possible combinations, but the outcomes are not that different. They all look kind of similar.</p><figure class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p" id="7c11" name="7c11"><img alt="Pile of all blue Legos" class="graf-image" data-height="3024" data-image-id="0*Y0sKT4MjKKwRDzhi" data-unsplash-photo-id="US9Tc9pKNBU" data-width="4032" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/0*Y0sKT4MjKKwRDzhi"/><figcaption class="imageCaption">Photo by <a class="markup--anchor markup--figure-anchor" data-href="https://unsplash.com/@ryanquintal?utm_source=medium&amp;utm_medium=referral" href="https://unsplash.com/@ryanquintal?utm_source=medium&amp;utm_medium=referral" rel="photo-creator noopener" target="_blank">Ryan Quintal</a> on <a class="markup--anchor markup--figure-anchor" data-href="https://unsplash.com?utm_source=medium&amp;utm_medium=referral" href="https://unsplash.com?utm_source=medium&amp;utm_medium=referral" rel="photo-source noopener" target="_blank">Unsplash</a></figcaption></figure><p class="graf graf--p graf-after--figure" id="62b8" name="62b8">But what if you bring in a Lego Friends set? Or some Duplo blocks? All of a sudden, the potential outcomes change quite drastically. Even more so if you relax the requirements of being Lego. What about Mega Blocks? Or building a wooden enclosure to which you could attach Legos? By adding in diverse pieces, you widely expand the universe of potential outcomes. Limiting your Diversity is done to your own detriment.</p><figure class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p" id="72ef" name="72ef"><img alt="Lego record store" class="graf-image" data-height="2634" data-image-id="0*8y1Wk_LEqqLIj8-N" data-unsplash-photo-id="JXIFjYVbAS8" data-width="3736" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/0*8y1Wk_LEqqLIj8-N"/><figcaption class="imageCaption">Photo by <a class="markup--anchor markup--figure-anchor" data-href="https://unsplash.com/@rhowerter?utm_source=medium&amp;utm_medium=referral" href="https://unsplash.com/@rhowerter?utm_source=medium&amp;utm_medium=referral" rel="photo-creator noopener" target="_blank">Ryan Howerter</a> on <a class="markup--anchor markup--figure-anchor" data-href="https://unsplash.com?utm_source=medium&amp;utm_medium=referral" href="https://unsplash.com?utm_source=medium&amp;utm_medium=referral" rel="photo-source noopener" target="_blank">Unsplash</a></figcaption></figure><p class="graf graf--p graf-after--figure graf--trailing" id="1b4a" name="1b4a">I am seeking out the most Diversity I can when I am trying to solve a problem now. It is way easier when you don’t limit the types of pieces you include.</p></div></div></section>
