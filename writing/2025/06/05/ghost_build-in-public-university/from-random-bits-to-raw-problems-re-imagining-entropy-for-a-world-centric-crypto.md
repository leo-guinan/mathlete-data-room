title: From Random Bits to Raw Problems: Re-imagining Entropy for a World-Centric Crypto

<blockquote><em>“A manifesto isn’t just a declaration—it’s a structured signal of lived problems.”</em> </blockquote><p>Most blockchains treat <strong>entropy</strong> as a sterile stream of random numbers: 256-bit secrets from <code>/dev/urandom</code>, beacons like drand, or validator lotteries. That machine entropy is crucial for signatures and consensus—but it says <em>nothing</em> about what really needs fixing outside the ledger.</p><p><strong>Temporal Crypto flips the telescope.</strong><br>Instead of “trust me, my RNG is unpredictable,” we ask: <strong>“Show me the world’s unanswered questions—and prove you saw them first.”</strong> Every newly documented problem becomes a micro-injector of uncertainty, feeding the network with fresh, <em>meaningful</em> entropy.</p><hr><h3 id="1-why-the-old-entropy-isn%E2%80%99t-enough">1. Why the Old Entropy Isn’t Enough</h3><p>Randomness from silicon protects against key guessing, but it <strong>assumes</strong> that whatever state we feed into the chain (prices, weather data, election results) is already true. That gap—the <em>oracle problem</em>—is where most exploits hide. We’re still trusting a human, an API, or a bridge to say “this is reality.”</p><hr><h3 id="2-entropy-as-evidence-the-manifesto-pipeline">2. Entropy as Evidence: The Manifesto Pipeline</h3>
